WHAT'S TRENDING

As countries, governments, and communities scramble to support the increase in global population, Smart Cities enabled by intelligent information and communication technologies (ICT) technologies have grown in prominence in recent times. Rapid urbanization coupled with ambitious targets set by countries to curb carbon emissions is creating a pressing need for smart cities to push efficiency and reduce environmental footprint. With unabated migration to urban areas, cities are confronting a host of issues including increased congestion, shortage of resources, and rising pollution levels. The need to find an efficient and sustainable solution to these issues is pushing focus onto smart cities. Growing environmental consciousness on part of governments and citizens and adoption of stringent regulations to reduce carbon footprint is another driving force for the smart cities market. The urgent need to replace aging infrastructure in developed nations with new and upgraded technologies, and infrastructure development initiatives being undertaken by governments of developing nations also bode well for the smart cities market.

Smart infrastructure such as smart grids and water networks helps in reducing wastages and in turn generates surpluses. Smart security and surveillance systems help curb crime even in tightly-packed urban areas. Smart traffic management helps in disciplining traffic even in the densest of cities. The implementation of open Wi-Fi networks provides the foundation for connected living and strong and agile community engagement. Some of the technologies playing vital roles as key enablers of the smart city concept include intelligent lighting, smart building controls, demand response, LED lighting, solar panels, fuel cells, wireless charging for automobiles, NFC, facial recognition, transportation sensors, wind turbines, and low power semiconductors, among others.

Smart cities can play an important role in reducing emissions by harnessing new technologies and adopting automation in applications such as traffic management, remote working, and pollution monitoring etc. Use of electric vehicles and autonomous cars are expected to contribute significantly towards reduction of fossil fuel emissions in the transportation sector. Similarly, use of better traffic management solutions is expected to result in less congestion in crowded cities, aid in traffic optimization, and reduce commute times. IoT-enabled smart traffic lights could deploy machine learning to facilitate efficient traffic control schemes. Mobility as a service (MaaS) enables efficient commuting of people and reduce number of cars on road. Bus service apps such as Beeline, employ IoT to develop routes based on community demand and transportation data analysis. Smart parking apps aid in reducing traffic congestion, cutting down fuel usage and lowering emission levels.

Supported by the latest technological developments in areas such as governance, energy, transport, and healthcare among others, the smart cities market is expected to pace forward in the forthcoming years. Technologies such as open data, Big Data, cognitive computing, Internet of Things (IoT) and cloud computing are proving to be beneficial to smart cities. Public private partnership models and growing investments by private and public entities are aiding the implementation of smart city projects. The increasing use of latest technologies supports in improving the infrastructure elements of a country, such as waste management, energy management, public safety and security, and urban mobility. The rising demand for smart utility and smart healthcare is also driving market gains. Advanced digital and mobile technologies are fueling demand smart healthcare solutions. Local authorities are concentrating on improving communication with citizens to provide priority to public healthcare via analysis of previous public health data for creation of policies, and also through health checkups. Further, the development and use of smart utility systems such as smart waste management system, smart meters, and smart grids has obtained considerable attention from governments for the integration of new-age smart utility applications, from the grass-root level of smart city development. Such aspects aid in reducing wastage and improving sustainability of cities.

  • 5G- With its revolutionary and transformative potential, 5G is set to be the lifeline of smart cities. The technology scores big over 4G in terms of data-transfer speed and latency, offering around 10 times the speed delivered by its predecessor. Compared to 4G, 5G network offers significant advantages in terms of faster speed, broader data bandwidth and low latency, all of which have the potential to transform various digital experiencing ranging from business applications to self-driving vehicles. The technology supports a comprehensive network of advanced sensors within infrastructure elements such as parking meters and traffic lights that connect and analyze data for optimizing real-world systems. 5G technology is capable of supportive massive number of connected devices across smart cities, which are associated with smart factories, smart homes, low-power machine-type communications, monitoring networks and end-users. 5G delivers the required framework of capabilities, use cases, services and technologies to help smart cities in managing these high-density systems.
  • Wi-Fi6 - Intended to support mesh-based communication in smart homes, Wi-Fi6 (IEEE 802.11ax) presents an effective companion of 5G technology. The latest version of Wi-Fi ensures optimal use of the spectrum by offering power-control techniques for eliminating interference among neighboring networks. Moreover, the higher-order 1024-QAM modulation scheme, Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access (OFDMA), and addition of up-link direction with down-link of MU-MIMO or MIMO allows an impressive throughput of around 6Gbps. The strategy also reliably improves power usage and support emerging security protocols like WPA3 and Target Wake Time
  • Blockchain - Smart cities are likely to increasingly bet on blockchain for ensuring secure transmission of data, resource sharing and smart logistics. The blockchain technology, in conjunction with the Internet of Things (IoT), holds the potential for making smart cities a reality. Sensors may be leveraged by municipalities for connecting traffic lights, vehicles, street signs and other objects to the IoT, thus facilitating traffic management and maximizing efficiency. Such an use of the blockchain technology holds the potential for reducing traffic congestion, vehicle emissions and commute times. The technology may also offer potential conveniences, including traffic ticket payment; vehicle parking and battery charging payments; and maintenance and accident monitoring services. Blockchain is anticipated to gain considerable prominence across smart cities, and receive support from leading companies such as IBM, Microsoft and Samsung.
  • Edge Computing: Edge computing is one of the vibrant elements behind the emergence of smart cities owing to its ability to enable plethora of new applications and support next-generation smart city solutions. The technology is anticipated to be extensively used by city governors and urban planners for leveraging emerging data-driven technology to find real solutions for pressing challenges. The increasing population density across cities is prompting authorities to exploit substantial data volumes for meaningful, real-time insights. Edge computing reduces the requirement to send real-time data to conventional datacenters by allowing data processing near to the data source. Connected devices and platforms such as autonomous vehicles come with desired processing capabilities for performing analysis, eliminating the need to transfer data to centralized clouds. Autonomous vehicles rely heavily on edge datacenters for using data in real-time. On the other hand, smart homes are generating significant amount of data regarding energy consumption, noise levels and pollution. The data is being processed by using edge datacenters.

Large volumes of data gathered across disparate local agencies from diverse sources in an urban environment set the stage for the integration of artificial intelligence (AI) in smart cities.   • Internet of Things:The Internet of Things (IoT) is emerging as one of the vital concepts in the development of smart cities. The potential for IoT is immense since sensor prices have declined by over 50% over the past decade, while bandwidth cost has also come down by 40 times over the past ten years. The rise of Internet of Things (IoT) has led to increasing implementation digital twins in smart cities ranging from urban planning to land-use optimization. Compared to Computer Aided Design CAD software used by city planners, digital twins offer a physical aspect to interact in addition to insights on designing and the design process. It enables users to generate simulations that guide their future plans. Construction of smart city using digital twin city is built data accumulated from sensors as well as the digital subsystems and technologies such as IoT, cloud computing, big data, and AI and 5G . There are a number of use cases for digital twins in the smart city space. Some of the application markets include connected devices such as autonomous drones, connected vehicles, and smart building management among others. In building information modelling (BIM) models, digital twins can be used as a platform across the value chain to integrate all data regarding the design and fabrication of a building.
  • Combining IoT and AI: The automated connectivity of IoT in smart cities is instrumental in cutting down public expenditures earmarked for infrastructure and provides new revenue opportunities. Combining IoT and AI provides excellent platforms and unlocks opportunities within smart cities including smart healthcare, autonomous vehicles and adaptive energy systems. Around 35 billion IoT connected devices and one billion cameras deployed on commercial buildings, infrastructure and government property are forecast to feed data to AI platforms by 2030. For example, smart street lighting features electric vehicle charging and also makes use of radar to detect traffic flows and adjust lighting levels accordingly, reducing energy consumption and cost. Smart waste and recycling management can optimize pickup efficiency and avoid overflow. Wearable devices and smartphone apps enable automated reporting of temperature, humidity, air quality and other environmental factors. The transition to AI guided transport includes intelligent transport systems such as self-driving vehicles, together with smart highways, intelligent traffic lights and smart parking garages will save time and fuel. Intelligent transport options could ease traffic congestion, improve public transport services and reduce pollution.
  • Big Data: With the number of connected devices forecast to grow rapidly, explosive growth of data is expected to present challenges for city authorities. In addition to this data-related revolution, modern-day cities are also being disrupted by factors such as climate change, sustainability, and inequity, challenging the manner in which cities are built and operated. Smart cities are implementing big data applications to intelligent management of infrastructure and natural resources, drive new efficiencies, promote sustainable urban planning, minimize wastage, facilitate effective spending and improve living standards.
  • One of the key trends seen in the smart cities market is the unification and sharing of data and control associated with a city, as against the earlier scenario when cities typically had disparate systems located in isolated silos. With highly commoditized and low cost hardware enabling massive storage and facilitating widespread adoption of the Cloud, city systems are now in a position to share data about centrally managed functions such as traffic as well as data such as emergency services locations and police accident notifications. Though such datasets in the past have proven to be useful even on an individual basis, their merger with other datasets (e.g. building management systems or power utilities) are ensuring that city managers have access to a more wider view. Such combined datasets ensure that existing functions can be operated in a more efficient manner, and hence bring to light additional possibilities for services that can improve lives of city residents.

  • Focus on Net Zero Energy Buildings (NZEB) - Net-zero energy buildings (NZEBs), often simply referred as zero energy buildings, have been increasingly gaining prominence across various geographical markets and regions as a mechanism for addressing climate change. These buildings utilize energy efficient technologies and renewable energy systems for consuming no more energy than generated onsite using renewable resources over a course of one year, essentially zeroing out on their own energy footprint. The idea of net-zero buildings that produce sufficient energy to meet their requirements has been attracting various governments, mainly in the US and Europe, for moving towards a sustainable future. Rather than acting as pure consumers, these buildings are capable of providing energy or other resources, leading to enhanced energy efficiency. Governments are making serious efforts to improve energy efficiency of buildings and focusing on a number of additional practices to achieve the goal. Stakeholders are focusing on on-site or distributed renewable resources and generation for supplementing and even replacing external resources. In addition to investing in smart grids, authorities are implementing energy storage options that can be used to store energy for later use. Various governments are using advanced techniques to improve building efficiency and reducing overall energy-related costs of these structures. In order to achieve high-performance and energy-efficient buildings, various stakeholders and decision makers are coming together for a coordinated action.
